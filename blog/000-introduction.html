Introduction

(or, What Are We Doing Here?)

It seems all blogs should have an introduction post, so here is ours! Since this will probably be one of the first posts you read here, I'll take the opportunity to tell you a little bit about myself and explain my plans for the blog.

First off...me! My name is Daniel Sissom. I am primarily an astrophysicist by training. I went to Vanderbilt for grad school and graduated with my doctorate in physics in 2015. I spent most of my time there throwing galaxies and black holes around in computers and watching how they evolved. If you like the gritty details in that kind of thing, check out my dissertation or my paper on the arXiv. I've also dabbled in nuclear physics at the now-dismantled HRIBF at Oak Ridge National Lab during my undergraduate summers at Tennessee Tech.

I say all that not to bore you with a résumé, but instead to give some indication of how I think about things. I gravitate (warning: astro pun) toward thinking about the big picture. I trust logic, even when it leads to counter-intuitive results. I reserve the right to be wrong. I love to argue if it means that someone is going to learn something (especially if that someone is me). Much of how I approach a topic stems from training as a scientist, and what I find easiest to talk about results from what I've found interesting and tools I've found useful along the way.

I am one of the (unfortunately, increasingly rare) examples of a scientist who is also a Christian. I've always found the intersection of science and religion a very interesting topic – a topic, I'm sure, that will form a non-negligible percentage of the blog posts here. I've been perpetually disappointed by how often Christians and the "scientifically-minded" are thought of as completely disparate groups, and, in fact, how much each group really does view the other as "the bad guys." Through some of the posts here, I hope to dispel as much of that nonsense as I can and show that we should all have more in common than you might think.

I anticipate that most of this blog will be able to be boiled down to trying to be a good thinker: approaching complex problems from first principles, using logic to make conclusions, referencing source material instead of cultural tradition, considering edge cases and ultimate ramifications, simplifying where possible, and asking why (a lot). Once these things become second nature, discussing just about anything becomes much more fruitful.

The other half of the site, the tutorials, will be a much more practical "how to do things" type of setup. I expect this to be, for the most part, computer oriented, but won't shy away from other topics if something interesting comes up. Topics will, naturally, tend to cover areas where I have some experience, so I should tell you some of the things I do. I am a security-conscious Linux user and a command line junkie, and I have primarily been a Python, C, and Bash programmer in the realms of astrophysical simulations and data analysis. I write papers with LaTeX and make (relatively simple) websites from scratch with HTML and CSS (using the Jade and SASS preprocessors). I maintain Arch Linux systems and curate large amounts of data with redundant backup schemes. I edit in Vim, version control everything with Git, and use Make and the age-old standard core utilities as an IDE. I take pictures with a DSLR and an iPhone and process the results with a completely FOSS pipeline. I experiment with OpenWRT and CJDNS and link wireless routers together into meshnets. And I sing! (Though that's not all that relavent to the tech tutorials...) If these are the kind of things you are interested in or you'd like to learn, then the tutorials are for you.

Whether you're here for the blog or the tutorials (or maybe even both), I hope this site will be useful to you. I encourage you to give both sections a try and see if there is anything interesting there for you. We are setting out on a journey together, and I bet we'll both learn something along the way.
